# Snacks — v0

Snacks is the system for pulling shared primitives into an app. One of Houston's core ideas is that
an app is built by composing existing, well-made primitives rather than re-deriving them per
project; snacks is the mechanism that delivers those primitives into an app and defines how the app
builds on them. It is written in Go.

Snacks is a delivery seam, not the primitives themselves. The capabilities an app pulls in — access
control, billing, comms, the shared data model, and the rest — are specified and owned by their own
platforms. Snacks is the single, uniform answer to "how does an app take a dependency on a primitive
and extend it," shared across all of them, so every app composes the platform the same way.

## Owned over pinned

A primitive is delivered as **owned code sitting on top of a pinned core**:

- **Owned.** The code lands in the app's own repo. The app owns it and edits it freely — its
  divergence affects no other app.
- **Pinned.** Upstream behavior the app depends on and does not edit. Uniformity is the point: the
  platform and every other app rely on its shape, so an app cannot change it in place.

These are not two separate catalogs. Every primitive places a **seam** between a pinned core and an
owned surface, and what defines a primitive is *where it puts that seam* — what the app builds
against versus what it is handed to rewrite. An owned surface is always built against a pinned core;
a fully-pinned primitive simply has no owned surface at all.

## Where the seam sits

What pushes a primitive toward pinned is a single question: **would an app's divergence break a
contract the platform and other apps rely on.** The more it would, the more of the primitive is
pinned. This places each category at a different depth of the seam:

- **Frontend components** sit mostly owned. A thin pinned core carries behavior and accessibility; a
  fat owned surface carries the markup, styling, and composition the app is expected to rewrite.
  Divergence here breaks nothing shared, so the app owns almost all of it.
- **Backend abstractions** sit higher. More of the primitive is pinned — the contract other
  platforms and the runtime depend on — and the app owns a thinner extension layer that configures
  and composes it.
- **Shared table and data models** are effectively all pinned, with no owned surface. Every app and
  the platform agree on their shape; an app editing them is exactly the drift the seam exists to
  prevent.

## The registry and the CLI

Snacks delivers the two tiers by two mechanisms, and a single primitive commonly uses both at once —
an owned surface vendored in, built against a pinned core pulled as a dependency.

- **Owned surfaces are vendored from a registry.** The snacks CLI reads a registry of primitives and
  copies a primitive's owned source into the app's module, where it becomes the app's code. The
  registry entry names the source to copy and the pinned core it is built against, so pulling one
  primitive brings its dependency with it.
- **Pinned cores are Go module dependencies.** They are installed and version-locked like any Go
  dependency and are never edited in place. A primitive with no owned surface — a shared data model,
  a hard platform contract — ships only this way, as a package the app depends on and does not
  vendor.

## Upgrades

The two tiers upgrade differently, matching how much of each the app owns. A pinned core upgrades by
bumping its version, the same as any dependency, and the app takes the new behavior as-is. An owned
surface, once vendored, is simply the app's own code: re-pulling it through the CLI writes a fresh
copy of the registry's current source, and the app reconciles that against whatever it changed the
same way it reconciles any change to its own files — through its own version control. Snacks
delivers the new source; the app owns the merge.

## Seam with the capability platforms

Snacks delivers primitives; it does not define their behavior. Each capability platform — access
control, billing, comms, the data layer — specifies what its primitive *is* and what its pinned
contract guarantees, and in doing so decides where its own seam sits: how much of it an app may own
and how much stays pinned. Snacks owns the uniform *way* that decision reaches an app: the registry,
the CLI, the owned/pinned split, and the shape of the seam every primitive declares. Scaffolding a
new app is the CLI pulling that app's baseline set of primitives from the registry, so an app starts
already composed from the same seams every other app is built on.

## Boundaries

Snacks owns delivery and nothing beneath it. It does not define what a primitive does — each
capability platform owns its primitive's behavior and its pinned contract. It does not provision or
run infrastructure, own application data, or perform application-level logic; it is the mechanism
that puts a primitive into an app's repo and dependency graph with a clear line between what the app
owns and what stays pinned. It is the seam that lets every Houston app be composed from shared
primitives the same way — nothing above that, nothing below it.
